History of French Cuisine

French cuisine is a style of cooking developed by people of French origin or by the French. French cuisine consists of cooking traditions and practices from France. French cuisine has developed over the centuries, influenced by many cultures around Spain, Italy, Switzerland, Germany and Belgium. Cheese and wine are an important part of French cuisine. French cuisine has contributed significantly to Western cuisine.

French food culture criteria are widely used in equine cookery schools and culinary education. In November 2010, French gastronomy was added to the lists of the world's "intangible cultural heritage" by UNESCO. There are French dishes that today are recognised as part of French national cuisine. A meal usually consists of hors d'oeuvre or starter, main course, cheese or dessert, sometimes salad served before cheese or dessert.

Ratatouille

Although we recognise it as the rat in the cartoon, it means slurry in French. It is a flavour discovered in the poor and poor times of France. Ratatouille recipe, which is proof of how much importance the French attach to taste even when there was poverty in the country, has now taken its place in many good restaurant menus, especially in France.

  • 2 tablespoons olive oil, divided
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 eggplant, cut into 1/2 inch cubes
  • 2 teaspoons dried parsley
  • salt to taste
  • 1 cup grated Parmesan cheese
  • 2 zucchini, sliced
  • 2 large tomatoes, chopped
  • 2 cups sliced fresh mushrooms
  • 1 large onion, sliced into rings
  • 1 green or red bell pepper, sliced

Une soupe à l'oignon

Onion soups have been popular at least as far back as Roman times. Throughout history, onions were considered food for the poor because they were abundant and easy to grow. The modern version of this soup was made in Paris, France, in the 18th century with broth and caramelised onions.

Éclair

One of the very few things known about eclairs is that they originated in France in the late 19th century. Many food historians suggest that éclairs were first made by Marie-Antoine Carême, the famous pastry cook of the French royal family. "Éclair" means a burst of lightning in French. It got its name from the fact that it is easily eaten in one bite.
